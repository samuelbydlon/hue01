Our Story

I started Hue in my garage in July of 2024.

Most people have a hard time understanding what Hue is at first, and that makes sense. It's closer to a bar than a traditional beverage popup. Hue is a reservation-based, fully table service experience with a curated menu of drinks and flavors you won't find anywhere else. Every part of the experience is designed with intention, from handmade or thrifted coasters to incense made by my friend Rain Lee, a Malaysia Barista Champion.

The name Hue comes from the idea that we all see color differently. I think the idea of home works the same way. Hue exists to celebrate those differences, not as good or bad, but simply as contrast.

I sat on the idea for Hue for over a year before finally acting on it. What started as a small, intimate experiment quickly grew. Friends brought friends, who brought their friends and so on. In our first six events, we served around 300 people out of my 400 square foot garage. A year and a half later, Hue evolved into a pop-up teahouse and experiential platform, serving over 1,700 people both here in the US and abroad, collaborating with artists, potters, chefs, musicians, and designers, while growing a community of thousands both in person and online.

The menu changed every two months and was inspired by my experiences in Asia and by a growing team of creatives who found Hue as their own version of home. Reservations included three beverages of your choice and a specialty pastry made by our in-house baker or a chef collaborator, with gluten-free and dairy-free options always available. Each gathering was designed with all five senses in mind. Light, sound, texture, scent, taste, and pacing all mattered. Evenings often included a DJ playing jazz and house, a small library of photo books from around the world, and when space allowed, screenings of films from Asian cinema.

At its core, Hue was about home. It's shaped by my own search for belonging as an international student and my desire to create that feeling for others — it pulls from many cultures without trying to be any one of them.

It's a third space: part teahouse, part living room, part gallery, and part community table. It still surprises me how often Hue connected people. Because of the space, we had to seat strangers together, and more often than not, they left having made new friends. In a time when so many experiences are built around isolation or distraction, creating a place where connection happened naturally felt rare, and deeply needed.
